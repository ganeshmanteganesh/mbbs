*   **Object3: Causes of Normocytic Normochromic Anemia**
    *   **Explanation:** A comprehensive list of conditions categorized by their primary mechanism:

            *   **Chronic renal failure:** Decreased production of erythropoietin, a hormone essential for RBC production, by the kidneys.
            *   **Chronic liver disease:** Can affect erythropoietin production, bone marrow function, and nutrient absorption.
            *   **Endocrine disorders:** Hormonal imbalances (e.g., Hypopituitarism, Hypothyroidism, Hypoadrenalism) can indirectly impair erythropoiesis.
            *   **Hematological disorders:**
                *   **Marrow hypoplasia/aplasia:** Bone marrow failure to produce adequate blood cells.
                *   **Myeloproliferative neoplasms (MPN) / Myelofibrosis:** Clonal disorders of hematopoietic stem cells that can lead to ineffective erythropoiesis or replacement of marrow with fibrous tissue.
                *   **Sideroblastic anemia:** Impaired heme synthesis, leading to ineffective erythropoiesis, though often microcytic/hypochromic, some forms can present as normocytic.
            *   **Expansion of plasma volume (Pregnancy):** Dilutional anemia where the total red cell mass is normal but concentrated in a larger plasma volume, leading to lower hemoglobin/hematocrit.
        *   **Increased red cell loss or destruction:**
            *   **Acute blood loss:** Direct loss of red cells from hemorrhage.
            *   **Hypersplenism:** An overactive spleen sequesters and destroys blood cells prematurely.
            *   **Hematological disorders:**
                *   **Hemoglobinopathies (e.g., sickle cell disease):** Inherited disorders affecting hemoglobin structure, leading to RBC rigidity and premature destruction (hemolysis).
                *   **Hereditary spherocytosis:** Inherited defect in RBC membrane proteins, leading to fragile, spherical RBCs prone to splenic destruction.
                *   **Glucose-6-Phosphate dehydrogenase deficiency (G6PD):** Genetic disorder making RBCs susceptible to oxidative stress and hemolysis.
            *   **Microangiopathic anemias (e.g., DIC, TTP, HUS):** Hemolysis due to mechanical damage to RBCs as they pass through obstructed or abnormal small blood vessels.
            *   **Autoimmune hemolytic anemia (AIHA):** The immune system mistakenly attacks and destroys the body's own red blood cells.
            *   **Paroxysmal nocturnal hemoglobinuria (PNH):** An acquired clonal disorder leading to increased RBC susceptibility to complement-mediated lysis.
