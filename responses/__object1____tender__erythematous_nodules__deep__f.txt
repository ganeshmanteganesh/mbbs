**Object1: Erythema Nodosum (EN)**

*   **Concept:** A form of **panniculitis**, an inflammatory condition affecting the subcutaneous fat.
*   **Key Features:** Characterized by the acute onset of tender, erythematous (red), firm, deep nodules, primarily located on the shins (pretibial region), often bilaterally and symmetrically.
*   **Associated Symptoms:** Commonly accompanied by systemic symptoms such as fever, malaise (general discomfort), arthralgia (joint pain), and myalgia (muscle pain).
*   **Resolution:** Lesions typically resolve spontaneously over weeks without scarring, often leaving temporary bruise-like discoloration (post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation) as they fade.
*   **Significance:** EN is often a reactive process, serving as an indicator of an underlying systemic condition, infection (e.g., strep, tuberculosis), medication reaction, or inflammatory disease (e.g., sarcoidosis, inflammatory bowel disease).

**Object2: Erythema Multiforme (EM) / Stevens-Johnson Syndrome (SJS) / Toxic Epidermal Necrolysis (TEN)**

*   **Concept:** A spectrum of acute, immune-mediated mucocutaneous (affecting skin and mucous membranes) hypersensitivity reactions, ranging from self-limited to severe and life-threatening.
*   **Erythema Multiforme (EM):**
    *   **Distinguishing Feature:** Presence of characteristic "target lesions" â€“ concentric rings of erythema (redness) and edema (swelling), often with a central vesicle or bulla, resembling an "iris" or "bull's eye."
    *   **Other Lesions:** May also include papules, vesicles, and bullae (blisters).
    *   **Distribution:** Commonly affects the extremities (especially palms and soles), face, and oral mucosa.
    *   **Etiology:** Often triggered by infections, predominantly Herpes Simplex Virus (HSV).
*   **Stevens-Johnson Syndrome (SJS) / Toxic Epidermal Necrolysis (TEN):**
    *   **Severity:** Represents the most severe forms of cutaneous adverse drug reactions, distinct from EM (though historically grouped). SJS involves less than 10% total body surface area (TBSA) epidermal detachment, while TEN involves over 30% TBSA (with SJS/TEN overlap 10-30%).
    *   **Pathology:** Characterized by extensive epidermal detachment (skin sloughing and blistering), often with severe, widespread mucosal involvement (oral, ocular, genital).
    *   **Etiology:** Primarily triggered by medications (e.g., sulfa drugs, anticonvulsants, NSAIDs).
    *   **Clinical Significance:** Life-threatening emergencies due to massive skin barrier loss, fluid and electrolyte imbalance, high risk of secondary infection (sepsis), and multi-organ failure.

**Object3: Urticaria and Angioedema**

*   **Concept:** Both are mast cell-mediated hypersensitivity reactions involving localized dermal or subcutaneous tissue swelling. They often coexist.
*   **Urticaria (Hives):**
    *   **Mechanism:** Involves superficial dermal edema due to localized vasodilation and increased vascular permeability in the superficial dermis.
    *   **Key Features:** Manifests as intensely pruritic (itchy), erythematous (red), raised wheals (hives) that are transient (typically lasting less than 24 hours in one spot) and migratory.
*   **Angioedema:**
    *   **Mechanism:** Involves deeper swelling of the dermis, subcutaneous tissue, or submucosa due to similar vascular changes in deeper layers.
    *   **Key Features:** Presents as localized, non-pitting swelling, often affecting the face (lips, eyelids), tongue, larynx, and genitals. It is often described as painful or burning rather than intensely itchy.
    *   **Clinical Significance:** While urticaria is generally benign, angioedema (especially laryngeal involvement) can lead to life-threatening airway obstruction, requiring urgent medical intervention. Both can be acute or chronic, triggered by allergens, drugs (e.g., ACE inhibitors), physical stimuli, or be idiopathic.
